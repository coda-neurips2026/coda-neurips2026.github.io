Overview

Rethinking the AI stack for the agentic era

AI efficiency has, until now, been optimized around a stateless abstraction: given a prompt, a foundation model emits a sequence of tokens under constraints such as latency, throughput, memory, and energy. The rapid rise of agentic foundation-model workloads breaks this abstraction. Modern agents maintain persistent state, reuse memory across steps, call external tools, act in environments, coordinate with other agents, and execute long-horizon tasks asynchronously. The efficiency bottleneck is migrating from a single inference request to persistent, autonomous execution that spans models, runtimes, compilers, serving systems, the memory hierarchy, and hardware.

The CoDA workshop convenes the machine learning, systems, compiler, and hardware communities around one question: how should the entire AI stack be redesigned when the dominant workload is no longer a single inference request, but a stateful, memory-augmented, tool-using, and potentially multi-agent trajectory? We frame the answer as closed-loop co-design across three coupled loops, plus the emerging role of autonomous optimization agents.

Loop 1

Runtime–Workload Co-Design

Agentic execution turns inference into a long-lived, bursty, stateful workload: cross-turn & cross-agent KV-cache reuse and eviction, paged/disaggregated memory, prefix caching, async & speculative tool execution, and multi-agent orchestration.

Loop 2

Model–System Co-Design

Architectures & training objectives that are natively efficient for agentic execution: memory-centric & sub-quadratic models, hybrid designs, adaptive-compute & elastic models, retrieval-aware models, and low-cost distillation into efficient backbones.

Loop 3

Hardware–Infrastructure Co-Design

Persistent autonomous workloads reshape the substrate they run on: hardware-aware kernels & attention, accelerator and memory-hierarchy design for stateful inference, disaggregated & heterogeneous serving, and elastic deployment from datacenter to edge.

Cross-cutting

Agents for Systems

Foundation-model agents that close these loops: profiling workloads, diagnosing bottlenecks, synthesizing & tuning GPU kernels, optimizing compilers, and proposing runtime policies — with attention to evaluation and safety of self-optimizing pipelines.

Why now?

2025–2026 marks an inflection point: agentic foundation models have moved from research demos to production, and inference — not training — is now the dominant and fastest-growing cost of deployed AI. These threads advance rapidly but in separate communities. Without a venue that deliberately couples them, the field risks fragmentation: locally optimal models that are globally inefficient, and benchmarks that reward the wrong quantity. This is the right forum and the right moment to establish shared problems, metrics, and abstractions for closed-loop co-design.

Call for Papers

Topics of interest

We invite papers, demos, and benchmarks across the agenda below. All accepted work is non-archival. Topics include, but are not limited to:

Runtime & Serving for Agents

  • KV-cache reuse, sharing, eviction & compression across turns and agents
  • Scheduling, batching & admission control for long-horizon, bursty trajectories
  • Asynchronous / speculative tool calls and tool-result caching
  • Multi-agent orchestration, routing & disaggregated (prefill/decode) serving

Efficient Models & Architectures

  • Memory-centric, sub-quadratic & hybrid architectures for long, stateful contexts
  • Adaptive-compute, elastic & many-in-one models matching compute to difficulty
  • Retrieval- & memory-augmented models; persistent and long-term state
  • Low-cost adaptation: distillation, compression, PEFT & upcycling

Hardware & Infrastructure Co-Design

  • Hardware-aware kernels, attention & operators for stateful inference
  • Accelerator, memory-hierarchy & interconnect design for persistent workloads
  • Heterogeneous, disaggregated & elastic serving from datacenter to edge

Agents for Systems (Autonomous Optimization)

  • LLM agents for kernel generation, compiler tuning & performance debugging
  • Autonomous workload profiling, bottleneck diagnosis & runtime-policy synthesis
  • Reliability, reproducibility & safety of self-optimizing systems

Efficient Reasoning & Test-Time Compute

  • Adaptive "thinking budget" & test-time compute allocation for long-horizon tasks
  • Combining test-time compute with quantization, speculative decoding & caching
  • Efficient reasoning on heterogeneous / resource-constrained devices

Evaluation, Benchmarking & Metrics

  • End-to-end, compute-aware metrics for full agent trajectories (latency, memory, energy, $/task)
  • Benchmarks & leaderboards for agentic serving, multi-agent systems & tool use
  • Quality–cost–reliability trade-offs under realistic deployment constraints

Submissions

How to submit

Format

We accept short papers, full papers, and demos / benchmarks. Submissions are handled on OpenReview, each receiving 3 reviews.

Non-archival

All accepted work is non-archival (no NeurIPS overlap). Please refrain from submitting already-finalized or previously published work.

Reviewing

We require at least one volunteer reviewer per submission to ensure coverage and timely, thoughtful reviews.

Mentorship

Three virtual mentorship sessions before the deadline help new researchers and underrepresented groups prepare high-quality submissions.
